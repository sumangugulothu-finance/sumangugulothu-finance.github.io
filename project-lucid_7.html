Background

It Started With a Number That Didn't Add Up

As part of my monthly headcount forecasting at Lucid Motors, I build burden rate models across multiple employee grades — from Grade 0 contractors to full-time salaried employees. Each grade carries a different cost burden, so accuracy in headcount is critical to getting payroll forecasts right.

During a routine month-end close, I noticed two signals that directly contradicted each other. First, headcount was higher than forecast. Normally that means overtime should decrease — more people sharing the workload means fewer extra hours per person. But instead, overtime costs were also rising. Both headcount AND overtime were going up at the same time. That should never happen. That contradiction triggered a deeper investigation.

The Anomaly
"Headcount was rising. Overtime should have dropped. Instead it was also rising. Both going up at the same time — that's impossible unless something is wrong."
Investigation

Digging Into the Data

01
Variance Presentation
Brought the headcount vs. dollar discrepancy to the forecast review meeting, presenting the variance across cost centers to senior leadership and business partners.
02
Second Red Flag — Overtime Rising Too
When headcount increases, overtime per person should naturally decrease — more workers sharing the same workload means fewer extra hours needed. But the data showed overtime costs were also climbing alongside headcount. Two numbers moving in the same direction when they should move opposite confirmed something structural was wrong.
03
HR Data Deep Dive
Requested granular employee data from HR across all grade levels. Built a headcount model breaking down active employees by grade, cost center, shift, and badge activity.
04
Pattern Recognition
While modeling badge entry and exit data, identified a recurring pattern: two badge taps registering at shift start, but only one badge tap at shift end. Across multiple cost centers, this pattern appeared consistently.
05
Root Cause Identified
Cross-referencing badge data with payroll confirmed it: operators were tapping a colleague's badge at the turnstile gate to register them as present — while that employee wasn't actually working. Payroll was processing pay for employees who weren't on the floor, inflating headcount costs.
06
Built Live Dashboard to Present Findings
To make the data undeniable and easy to act on, I built two dashboards — a live headcount tracking dashboard showing real-time headcount versus forecast by cost center and grade, and a spend tracking dashboard monitoring overtime and labor costs against budget. These dashboards became the single source of truth presented to leadership and business partners, making the variance pattern visually clear and impossible to dispute.
07
Business Partner Collaboration
Partnered with Operations, HR, and IT leadership across each cost center to validate findings, walk through the data, and build a remediation plan that was fair, accurate, and operationally viable.
08
System Fix — Okta Verification
Worked with IT to implement Okta-based identity verification at badge entry points, ensuring each badge tap required active authentication tied to the actual employee. Badge sharing was eliminated at the system level.

Key Takeaway

What This Project Taught Me

The most valuable financial insights don't always come from spreadsheets — they come from asking why a number doesn't make sense and being willing to follow the data wherever it leads. This project required me to connect financial modeling, operational knowledge, HR data, and cross-functional relationships to solve a problem that wasn't obvious from the surface.

It reinforced that strong FP&A isn't just about building accurate forecasts — it's about being curious enough to investigate when reality doesn't match the model.
